# Author Voice Reference

Apply the selected author's voice **throughout the entire post** — not just the opening. The voice must be consistent and recognizable in sentence rhythm, vocabulary register, paragraph length, and structural habits. Think of it as method acting, not costume.

## Russian Authors

---

### 1. Варламов

**Signature feel:** Urban flaneur with a sharp tongue. Every idea is grounded in a specific place, a specific broken thing, a specific bureaucratic absurdity witnessed that morning.

**Sentence rhythm:** Short. Declarative. Then a longer one that builds context. Then short again.

**Vocabulary:** Street-level, colloquial but not crude. Uses "вот" and "смотрите" as hooks. Never abstract — always a street name, a city, a specific ugly building.

**Structural habits:**
- Opens with a physical observation or a micro-scene
- Pivots to the systemic problem behind the small detail
- Ends with a blunt verdict, often ironic

**Tone:** Provocative but not cruel. The reader feels they're walking beside him, noticing things they used to ignore.

**Avoid:** Long philosophical tangents, academic vocabulary, polite hedging.

**Example register:**
> Я был в Саратове. Там снесли старый рынок и построили парковку. Никто не спрашивал жителей. Никто никогда не спрашивает.

---

### 2. Дубинин (Послезавтра)

**Signature feel:** The calm futurist. Explains tomorrow's world with the patience of a professor who genuinely loves his students.

**Sentence rhythm:** Medium-length sentences. Flows steadily, like a well-paced lecture. No staccato bursts — more like a river than a waterfall.

**Vocabulary:** Tech-forward but accessible. Translates complex systems into human-scale consequences. Uses analogies heavily.

**Structural habits:**
- Opens with a near-future scenario stated as near-fact
- Builds the evidence layer by layer
- Ends with a quietly optimistic "here's what this means for you"

**Tone:** Thoughtful, assured, never alarmist. The reader feels smart for reading it.

**Avoid:** Sarcasm, political provocation, clickbait framing.

**Example register:**
> Через десять лет большинство людей не будут выбирать профессию. Профессия будет выбирать их — на основе данных, которые они оставляют каждый день.

---

### 3. Пивоваров (Редакция)

**Signature feel:** The investigative documentarian. Tells you a story, then pulls back the curtain on what's really happening behind it.

**Sentence rhythm:** Varied — short punchy setups, then longer explanatory passages. Builds narrative suspense.

**Vocabulary:** Journalistic, precise, occasionally wry. Uses rhetorical questions to guide the reader.

**Structural habits:**
- Opens with a character or a specific event
- Introduces the "official version" then dismantles it
- Uses "но есть одна проблема" or equivalent to pivot
- Ends with an open question or unsettling implication

**Tone:** Engaged, serious, with dry wit. Reader feels they're watching a premium documentary.

**Avoid:** Rants, opinion without evidence, emotional manipulation.

**Example register:**
> В 2019 году чиновник подписал документ. Всего один. Никто не заметил. Но именно этот документ изменил то, как работает целая отрасль.

---

### 4. Максим Кац

**Signature feel:** The rational explainer. Breaks down political and urban systems with data, patience, and a slight "why don't more people know this?" energy.

**Sentence rhythm:** Clear, methodical. Numbered or logical progressions even in prose form. No wasted words.

**Vocabulary:** Evidence-based. References studies, statistics, international comparisons. Accessible but never dumbed down.

**Structural habits:**
- Opens by stating what most people believe
- Systematically shows why that belief is wrong or incomplete
- Proposes a better framework, backed by examples from other cities/countries
- Ends with a practical implication

**Tone:** Constructive, slightly pedagogical, optimistic about rational solutions.

**Avoid:** Emotional language, vague claims, conspiracy framing.

**Example register:**
> Большинство людей думают, что пробки — это следствие слишком большого числа машин. Это неверно. Пробки — это следствие неправильного ценообразования на дорожное пространство.

---

### 5. Александр Файб

**Signature feel:** Personal-development meets street philosophy. Mixes self-reflection, practical frameworks, and honest vulnerability.

**Sentence rhythm:** Conversational, medium-length. Feels like a long voice message from a smart friend.

**Vocabulary:** Modern, relatable, occasionally English loanwords natural to the Russian internet generation.

**Structural habits:**
- Opens with a personal confession or moment of failure
- Extracts a universal principle from it
- Gives practical takeaways without being preachy
- Ends on an encouraging, forward-looking note

**Tone:** Warm, direct, self-aware. Reader thinks "this person gets it."

**Avoid:** Guru posturing, toxic positivity, corporate jargon.

**Example register:**
> Я облажался. Потратил три месяца на проект, который никому не был нужен. И вот что я понял из этого.

---

### 6. Евгений Киселев

**Signature feel:** The elder statesman of Russian journalism. Measured, historical, gives every story its geopolitical weight.

**Sentence rhythm:** Long, carefully constructed sentences. Formal but not stiff. The pace of someone who has seen a great deal and chooses words accordingly.

**Vocabulary:** Elevated, classical Russian register. References history, precedent, international parallels.

**Structural habits:**
- Opens with historical context
- Draws a line from past to present
- Names the structural forces at play
- Ends with a sober assessment, rarely optimistic

**Tone:** Authoritative, measured, occasionally mournful. Reader feels they're reading history as it happens.

**Avoid:** Slang, irony, quick takes, listicle energy.

**Example register:**
> История знает немало примеров того, как государства, убеждённые в собственной правоте, совершали ошибки, цена которых измерялась десятилетиями.

---

### 7. Леонид Парфёнов

**Signature feel:** The aesthete-historian. Makes culture and history feel urgent and glamorous simultaneously.

**Sentence rhythm:** Rhythmic, almost musical. Short aphoristic lines alternate with rich descriptive passages.

**Vocabulary:** Precise, literary, culturally layered. Loves proper nouns — names, places, dates used like brushstrokes.

**Structural habits:**
- Opens with a vivid cultural or historical image
- Weaves detail and significance together tightly
- Uses the specific to illuminate the universal
- Ends with a resonant, slightly poetic statement

**Tone:** Elegant, confident, celebratory of human achievement. Reader feels culturally enriched.

**Avoid:** Political polemic, vulgarity, empty abstractions.

**Example register:**
> 1961 год. Гагарин улыбается на весь мир. Эта улыбка — не просто эмоция. Это государственный символ, тщательно отобранный из сотен кандидатов.

---

### 8. Стас Белковский

**Signature feel:** The intellectual provocateur. Combines deep political analysis with deliberate paradox and dark humour.

**Sentence rhythm:** Unpredictable. Builds a logical case, then detonates it with an unexpected line.

**Vocabulary:** Erudite, ironic, occasionally theatrical. Quotes philosophy and pop culture in the same breath.

**Structural habits:**
- Opens with a claim that sounds outrageous but will be proven
- Builds the argument with apparent seriousness
- Introduces a twist that reframes everything
- Ends with a bitter or absurdist conclusion

**Tone:** Sardonic, brilliant, unsettling. Reader is never quite comfortable — and can't stop reading.

**Avoid:** Sentimentality, straightforward optimism, safe conclusions.

**Example register:**
> Путин не правит Россией. Россия правит Путиным. Разница принципиальная, и именно её не понимают все западные аналитики.

---

### 9. Соколовский

**Signature feel:** The young edgelord turned thoughtful commentator. Provocative framing, but real substance underneath.

**Sentence rhythm:** Fast, punchy. Short bursts. High energy. Feels like a video essay transcript.

**Vocabulary:** Internet-native Russian. Direct, sometimes blunt, unexpectedly well-researched.

**Structural habits:**
- Opens with a hot take stated as plain fact
- Immediately backs it up with evidence
- Anticipates and preempts objections
- Ends with a challenge to the reader

**Tone:** Confident, slightly combative, energetic. Reader either agrees loudly or wants to argue back — both are wins.

**Avoid:** Timidity, over-qualification, academic hedging.

**Example register:**
> Большинство людей, которые говорят о здоровом образе жизни, не знают базовой физиологии. Вообще. И вот почему это проблема.

---

### 10. Доктор Комаровский

**Signature feel:** The trusted family doctor who cuts through medical myths with warmth and evidence.

**Sentence rhythm:** Clear, steady, medium-length. Like a patient explanation in a good consultation.

**Vocabulary:** Medical but always translated for parents. Never condescending. Uses analogies from everyday life.

**Structural habits:**
- Opens by naming the myth or fear most parents have
- Dismantles it gently but firmly with science
- Offers the practical, calm alternative
- Ends with reassurance and a clear action

**Tone:** Warm, authoritative, slightly humorous. Reader feels relieved and informed.

**Avoid:** Alarmism, medical jargon left unexplained, vague reassurances without substance.

**Example register:**
> Зелёные сопли — это не признак бактериальной инфекции. Это нормальная стадия любого насморка. Антибиотики здесь не нужны. Вот что нужно.

## English Authors

---

### 1. Giulia Enders

**Signature feel:** Science made intimate and slightly funny. Makes the body feel like a fascinating stranger worth getting to know.

**Sentence rhythm:** Conversational, warm, medium-length with playful asides. Reads like a smart friend explaining biology over coffee.

**Vocabulary:** Accessible science. Introduces technical terms then immediately humanizes them. Uses "we" to include the reader in the body's story.

**Structural habits:**
- Opens with a surprising or slightly taboo biological fact
- Explains the mechanism with clarity and delight
- Connects it to everyday experience
- Ends with a feeling of wonder or affection for one's own body

**Tone:** Curious, warm, gently irreverent. Reader finishes feeling both smarter and more comfortable in their skin.

**Avoid:** Clinical coldness, fear-based health framing, unnecessary complexity.

**Example register:**
> Your gut has a nervous system of its own. It doesn't ask your brain for permission. It just decides — and most of the time, it decides correctly.

---

### 2. Alex Hormozi

**Signature feel:** The no-BS business operator. Every sentence earns its place. Zero fluff, maximum density of useful information.

**Sentence rhythm:** Short, punchy, declarative. Occasionally one-word sentences. Then a longer explanatory line. Repeat.

**Vocabulary:** Business-first. Direct. Uses specific numbers and real examples. "Most people" and "here's the truth" are recurring frames.

**Structural habits:**
- Opens with a contrarian business truth stated flatly
- Immediately backs it with a mechanism or data point
- Gives a replicable framework
- Ends with a challenge or a call to action that respects the reader's intelligence

**Tone:** Blunt, confident, generous with knowledge. Reader feels they got $1,000 of advice for free.

**Avoid:** Corporate softness, vague inspiration, excessive hedging.

**Example register:**
> Most businesses don't have a marketing problem. They have an offer problem. Fix the offer. The marketing becomes easy.

---

### 3. Sabri Suby

**Signature feel:** The direct-response copywriter. Every line is engineered to keep the reader moving forward. Urgency is always present, never fake.

**Sentence rhythm:** Varied for psychological effect. Short hooks. Medium explanations. Long builds of desire. Short close.

**Vocabulary:** Sales-aware but not sleazy. Uses the reader's pain points as the entry point. Power words deployed purposefully, not scattered randomly.

**Structural habits:**
- Opens by naming the reader's exact frustration or desire
- Agitates the problem (makes it feel urgent and real)
- Introduces the insight or solution as a revelation
- Ends with clear next step or stakes

**Tone:** Urgent, empathetic, persuasive. Reader feels understood before they feel sold to.

**Avoid:** Passive voice, abstract benefits, gentle indirection.

**Example register:**
> You already know what's wrong. You've known for months. The question isn't what to fix — it's why you haven't fixed it yet. Let's talk about that.

---

### 4. Robert Kiyosaki

**Signature feel:** The contrarian financial philosopher. Frames everything as a battle between mindsets, not methods.

**Sentence rhythm:** Simple, repetitive for emphasis. Uses contrast structures heavily: "The poor do X. The rich do Y."

**Vocabulary:** Plain, accessible. Deliberately avoids financial jargon to make the point that the system is designed to confuse you.

**Structural habits:**
- Opens with a story (often his "rich dad" vs "poor dad" frame)
- Extracts a mindset lesson from the story
- Challenges conventional financial wisdom directly
- Ends with a reframe that makes the reader question what they were taught

**Tone:** Provocative, paternalistic in a motivating way. Reader feels they've been let in on a secret the school system hid.

**Avoid:** Complexity, nuance overload, academic framing.

**Example register:**
> My poor dad told me to get a good job. My rich dad told me to build something that gives you jobs. I listened to the wrong man for too long.

---

### 5. Malcolm Gladwell

**Signature feel:** The master of the "surprising truth hiding in plain sight." Turns social science research into narrative page-turners.

**Sentence rhythm:** Long, flowing, story-first. Builds slowly and deliberately. The payoff is always earned by the journey.

**Vocabulary:** Literary non-fiction register. Precise but never dry. Names are important — real people anchor abstract ideas.

**Structural habits:**
- Opens with a specific story that seems unrelated to the thesis
- Introduces the research or concept mid-way
- Shows how the opening story was actually the thesis all along
- Ends by expanding the implication outward to society

**Tone:** Curious, slightly conspiratorial, always respectful of the reader's intelligence.

**Avoid:** Bullet-point thinking, quick conclusions, oversimplification (ironic given the criticism — but the *voice* avoids it).

**Example register:**
> In the winter of 1987, a researcher at the University of Michigan made an observation so small that she almost didn't write it down. She should have written it down sooner.

---

### 6. Michael Greger

**Signature feel:** The evidence-obsessed physician who wants you to live longer and won't stop citing studies to help you do it.

**Sentence rhythm:** Medium, information-dense. Each sentence carries data or a finding. Occasional dry wit punctuates the science.

**Vocabulary:** Medical and nutritional science, always explained. Uses "research suggests," "studies show," "the data indicates" as natural scaffolding.

**Structural habits:**
- Opens with a health claim most people haven't questioned
- Cites the research that challenges or confirms it
- Explains the mechanism in accessible terms
- Ends with a practical dietary or lifestyle implication

**Tone:** Earnest, slightly evangelical about evidence, warmly nerdy. Reader feels they've just read a very readable research paper.

**Avoid:** Anecdote over evidence, fear-mongering, supplement hawking.

**Example register:**
> A 2019 meta-analysis of forty-three studies found that increasing daily fiber by just ten grams reduced cardiovascular mortality by seventeen percent. That is not a small number.

---

### 7. Nassim Nicholas Taleb

**Signature feel:** The intellectual contrarian who has no patience for people who mistake maps for territory.

**Sentence rhythm:** Aphoristic. Short, dense, loaded sentences. Then longer explanatory bursts. Then back to an aphorism. Highly quotable.

**Vocabulary:** Erudite, technical (probability, statistics, philosophy) mixed with blunt street-smart plain speech. Never academic for its own sake.

**Structural habits:**
- Opens with a paradox or a provocation stated as obvious truth
- Dismantles a widely-held belief using probability or historical examples
- Introduces his framework (fragility, optionality, skin in the game)
- Ends with an implication that challenges how the reader lives or thinks

**Tone:** Combative, brilliant, occasionally arrogant — but the arrogance is usually backed up. Reader either loves it or is infuriated into thinking harder.

**Avoid:** Wishy-washy hedging, deference to credentials, gentle conclusions.

**Example register:**
> The people most confident about the future are the ones who understand it least. Uncertainty is not a problem to be solved. It is the condition of being alive.

---

### 8. Simon Whistler (VisualPolitics)

**Signature feel:** The engaging explainer of geopolitics and economics for intelligent non-specialists. Makes complex systems legible without oversimplifying them.

**Sentence rhythm:** Clear, measured, varied. Feels like a well-scripted documentary narration — designed to be followed without notes.

**Vocabulary:** International relations, economics, history — but always translated into plain stakes. "What does this mean for ordinary people?" is the constant underlying question.

**Structural habits:**
- Opens with a geopolitical or economic puzzle framed as a question
- Builds the context layer by layer (historical, structural, current)
- Names the competing forces and their incentives clearly
- Ends with a balanced assessment of likely outcomes

**Tone:** Authoritative, fair-minded, intellectually curious. Reader feels geopolitically literate by the end.

**Avoid:** Partisan framing, oversimplification, dramatic clickbait conclusions.

**Example register:**
> To understand why this decision was made, you need to understand what happened forty years ago — and why the people making it today were shaped by that history whether they know it or not.
